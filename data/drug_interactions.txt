# Drug Interactions Reference Guide
# Source: General Pharmacology Reference (Educational Use Only)
# Disclaimer: Always consult a pharmacist or physician before taking any medications.

=== CRITICAL DRUG INTERACTIONS ===

WARFARIN (Blood Thinner) Interactions:
WARNING: Warfarin has a narrow therapeutic index. Many drugs and foods can increase bleeding risk or reduce effectiveness.

Drugs that INCREASE bleeding risk (increase INR):
- Aspirin and NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen, diclofenac) — significantly increase bleeding risk
- Fluconazole, metronidazole, ciprofloxacin, clarithromycin — inhibit warfarin metabolism
- Amiodarone — potent inhibitor; may increase INR by 30-50%
- Statins (especially fluvastatin, lovastatin) — modest INR increases
- SSRIs (fluoxetine, sertraline) — inhibit platelet function, increase bleeding risk
- Acetaminophen (paracetamol) — can increase INR at doses >2g/day; safer than NSAIDs but still requires monitoring

Drugs that DECREASE warfarin effect (decrease INR):
- Rifampin — powerful inducer, can reduce warfarin effect by 50%+
- Carbamazepine, phenytoin, phenobarbital — enzyme inducers
- St. John's Wort — herbal supplement, significant interaction
- Griseofulvin, nafcillin, cholestyramine

Food interactions:
- Vitamin K-rich foods (spinach, kale, broccoli, Brussels sprouts) — decrease warfarin effect
- Cranberry juice — increases warfarin effect
- Grapefruit — modest interaction

Monitoring: Frequent INR checks whenever medications are added, changed, or stopped

STATINS (Cholesterol Medications) Interactions:
Common statins: atorvastatin, simvastatin, rosuvastatin, pravastatin, lovastatin

Interactions increasing risk of myopathy/rhabdomyolysis:
- Fibrates (gemfibrozil especially) — avoid combination; fenofibrate safer
- Niacin at high doses — increased myopathy risk
- Cyclosporine — greatly increases statin levels
- Azole antifungals (itraconazole, ketoconazole) — inhibit CYP3A4
- HIV protease inhibitors — significantly increase statin levels
- Macrolide antibiotics (erythromycin, clarithromycin) — CYP3A4 inhibitors
- Amiodarone — increases simvastatin levels; limit simvastatin dose to 20mg

Grapefruit interactions:
- Grapefruit and grapefruit juice — inhibit CYP3A4 metabolism of simvastatin, atorvastatin, lovastatin
- Rosuvastatin and pravastatin NOT significantly affected by grapefruit

Simvastatin dose limits with interacting drugs:
- With amiodarone: max 20mg/day
- With amlodipine or ranolazine: max 20mg/day
- With verapamil or diltiazem: max 10mg/day
- Avoid simvastatin with gemfibrozil, cyclosporine, danazol

METFORMIN Interactions:
- Iodinated contrast media — hold metformin before/after procedures with IV contrast (risk of lactic acidosis); typically hold 48 hours after
- Alcohol — increased risk of lactic acidosis; avoid excessive alcohol
- Cimetidine — increases metformin levels by reducing renal elimination
- Topiramate, dolutegravir — may increase metformin levels
- Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (topiramate, acetazolamide) — increased lactic acidosis risk

Contraindications (hold metformin):
- eGFR < 30 mL/min — contraindicated; reduce dose for eGFR 30-45
- Active/progressive hepatic disease
- IV contrast administration
- Surgery requiring anesthesia (temporarily hold)

---

ACE INHIBITORS and ARBs Interactions:
Common ACE inhibitors: lisinopril, enalapril, ramipril, perindopril
Common ARBs: losartan, valsartan, irbesartan, candesartan

Key interactions:
- Potassium supplements / potassium-sparing diuretics (spironolactone, amiloride) — risk of hyperkalemia; requires monitoring
- NSAIDs — reduce antihypertensive effect and increase risk of acute kidney injury; avoid combination
- Lithium — ACE inhibitors increase lithium levels, risk of toxicity
- Aliskiren (direct renin inhibitor) — avoid dual RAAS blockade; increased risk of hypotension, hyperkalemia, renal impairment
- ACE inhibitor + ARB combination — generally avoided; dual RAAS blockade not recommended
- Trimethoprim — acts like potassium-sparing diuretic, hyperkalemia risk

Side effects to monitor:
- ACE inhibitors: Dry cough (10-20% of patients), angioedema (rare but serious)
- ARBs: Generally better tolerated; lower cough incidence

---

BETA-BLOCKERS Interactions:
Common beta-blockers: metoprolol, atenolol, carvedilol, bisoprolol, propranolol

Key interactions:
- Verapamil and diltiazem (non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers) — additive negative chronotropic and dromotropic effects; risk of bradycardia and heart block; avoid IV combination
- Clonidine — abrupt clonidine withdrawal while on beta-blocker → severe hypertension; taper clonidine first
- Insulin and antidiabetic drugs — beta-blockers mask hypoglycemia symptoms (except sweating); may prolong hypoglycemia
- NSAIDs — reduce antihypertensive effect
- Digoxin — additive AV node suppression; risk of bradycardia and heart block
- Antidepressants (TCAs, MAOIs) — risk of severe hypertension

Important: Never abruptly stop beta-blockers — rebound hypertension and angina risk; taper gradually

---

ANTIBIOTICS — Key Interactions:

FLUOROQUINOLONES (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin, moxifloxacin):
- Antacids, calcium, magnesium, aluminum, iron, zinc — reduce fluoroquinolone absorption; take fluoroquinolone 2 hours before or 4-6 hours after
- Warfarin — increase anticoagulant effect; monitor INR closely
- QT-prolonging drugs — additive QT prolongation risk; avoid with antiarrhythmics, antipsychotics
- NSAIDs — increased CNS side effects, seizure risk
- Theophylline — ciprofloxacin increases theophylline levels; reduce dose and monitor

MACROLIDES (erythromycin, clarithromycin, azithromycin):
- QT prolongation — significant risk with other QT-prolonging drugs; erythromycin highest risk
- Statins — CYP3A4 inhibition; increased myopathy risk (avoid with simvastatin)
- Warfarin — increase INR; monitor closely
- Digoxin — clarithromycin increases digoxin levels; monitor for toxicity
- Colchicine — clarithromycin greatly increases colchicine levels; serious toxicity risk

TETRACYCLINES (doxycycline, tetracycline):
- Divalent cations (calcium, magnesium, aluminum, iron, zinc) — reduce absorption; take 1-2 hours before or after
- Dairy products — reduce tetracycline (not doxycycline) absorption significantly
- Warfarin — increase anticoagulant effect
- Oral contraceptives — theoretical interaction; use backup contraception (low evidence but common practice)
- Retinoids (isotretinoin) — avoid combination; additive risk of intracranial hypertension (pseudotumor cerebri)

TRIMETHOPRIM-SULFAMETHOXAZOLE (TMP-SMX / Bactrim):
- Warfarin — significant increase in INR; reduce warfarin dose by 25-50%
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs/potassium-sparing diuretics — hyperkalemia risk
- Methotrexate — increases methotrexate toxicity
- Phenytoin — inhibits phenytoin metabolism; monitor levels

PROTON PUMP INHIBITORS (PPIs) Interactions:
Common PPIs: omeprazole, pantoprazole, lansoprazole, esomeprazole, rabeprazole

- Clopidogrel — omeprazole and esomeprazole reduce clopidogrel antiplatelet effect (CYP2C19 inhibition); use pantoprazole or rabeprazole if PPI needed
- Methotrexate — PPIs reduce renal clearance; risk of methotrexate toxicity
- HIV antiretrovirals (atazanavir, rilpivirine) — PPIs reduce absorption; avoid combination
- Iron and calcium — PPIs reduce absorption (acid-dependent)
- Vitamin B12 — long-term PPI use can reduce B12 absorption
- Azole antifungals (ketoconazole, itraconazole) — reduced absorption; separate doses or use different antifungal

Long-term PPI risks:
- Hypomagnesemia (> 1 year use)
- Increased risk of C. difficile infection
- Possible increased risk of bone fractures with high-dose long-term use

---

SSRI/SNRI ANTIDEPRESSANTS Interactions:
Common SSRIs: fluoxetine, sertraline, escitalopram, paroxetine, citalopram
Common SNRIs: venlafaxine, duloxetine, desvenlafaxine

SEROTONIN SYNDROME — Most critical interaction:
Risk with combinations of:
- MAO inhibitors (phenelzine, tranylcypromine, selegiline) — CONTRAINDICATED; wait 14 days between MAOI and SSRI
- Linezolid (antibiotic with MAOI activity) — contraindicated with SSRIs
- Tramadol, fentanyl, meperidine — increased serotonin syndrome risk
- Triptans — theoretical serotonin syndrome risk; generally acceptable but monitor
- St. John's Wort — serotonin syndrome risk
- Lithium — additive serotonergic effects

Serotonin Syndrome Signs: Agitation, confusion, rapid heart rate, high blood pressure, dilated pupils, muscle twitching, loss of coordination, high body temperature, diarrhea

QT Prolongation:
- Citalopram and escitalopram — dose-dependent QT prolongation; avoid high doses and QT-prolonging drugs

Bleeding risk:
- NSAIDs + SSRIs — increased upper GI bleeding risk (inhibit platelet aggregation)
- Warfarin + SSRIs — increased bleeding risk

CYP450 interactions (fluoxetine and paroxetine are potent CYP2D6 inhibitors):
- Tramadol — reduced analgesic effect and increased seizure risk
- Codeine — reduced conversion to morphine; reduced analgesic effect
- Tamoxifen — paroxetine/fluoxetine reduce tamoxifen effectiveness; avoid combination
- TCAs — increased TCA levels

---

OPIOID ANALGESICS Interactions:
Common opioids: oxycodone, hydrocodone, codeine, tramadol, fentanyl, morphine, methadone

CNS depression — additive/synergistic effects:
- Benzodiazepines — BLACK BOX WARNING: additive respiratory depression, increased risk of overdose death
- Alcohol — increased CNS and respiratory depression
- Gabapentin/pregabalin — increased respiratory depression risk
- Muscle relaxants — additive CNS depression
- Antihistamines, antipsychotics, TCAs — additive sedation

Methadone-specific interactions:
- QT prolongation — significant concern; many drugs prolong QT interval
- Rifampin — dramatically reduces methadone levels; withdrawal risk
- HIV antiretrovirals — many complex interactions

Tramadol-specific:
- Serotonin syndrome risk (see SSRIs above)
- Seizure threshold lowering — avoid with other drugs that lower seizure threshold
- MAOIs — contraindicated

---

ORAL CONTRACEPTIVES Interactions:
Drugs that REDUCE contraceptive effectiveness:
- Enzyme-inducing antiepileptics: rifampin, carbamazepine, phenytoin, oxcarbazepine, topiramate (>200mg/day)
- Rifampin (and rifabutin to a lesser extent)
- St. John's Wort
- Griseofulvin (antifungal)
- Action: Use additional contraception (condoms) during use and for 28 days after stopping enzyme inducers

Drugs AFFECTED by oral contraceptives:
- Warfarin — may decrease or increase anticoagulant effect; monitor INR
- Lamotrigine — oral contraceptives reduce lamotrigine levels significantly (50%); seizure risk; adjust dose
- Cyclosporine — oral contraceptives may increase cyclosporine levels

---

DIGOXIN Interactions:
- P-glycoprotein inhibitors (amiodarone, quinidine, verapamil, clarithromycin, itraconazole, spironolactone) — increase digoxin levels; toxicity risk
- Diuretics (loop and thiazide) — hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia increase digoxin toxicity risk
- NSAIDs — reduce renal clearance; increase digoxin levels
- Antacids, cholestyramine, kaolin-pectin — reduce digoxin absorption

Digoxin Toxicity Signs:
- GI: Nausea, vomiting, anorexia, abdominal pain
- Cardiac: Bradycardia, heart block, various arrhythmias
- Visual: Blurred vision, yellow-green halos, photophobia
- Neurological: Confusion, fatigue, weakness, delirium

=== MEDICATION SAFETY PRINCIPLES ===

HIGH-ALERT MEDICATIONS (Require special caution):
- Anticoagulants: Warfarin, heparin, DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran)
- Insulin: Risk of hypoglycemia; double-check doses
- Opioids: Respiratory depression risk; start low, go slow
- Concentrated electrolytes: IV potassium chloride — never give undiluted IV push
- Chemotherapy agents: Narrow therapeutic index
- Antiepileptics: Narrow therapeutic range; monitor levels

BEERS CRITERIA — Medications to Avoid in Elderly (≥65 years):
- Benzodiazepines — increased fall/fracture risk, cognitive impairment
- First-generation antihistamines (diphenhydramine/Benadryl) — anticholinergic effects
- NSAIDs — GI bleeding, fluid retention, renal impairment
- Antipsychotics for dementia-related behavioral issues — increased mortality risk
- Tricyclic antidepressants — anticholinergic, orthostatic hypotension
- Muscle relaxants (cyclobenzaprine) — excessive sedation, anticholinergic effects

=== IMPORTANT DISCLAIMER ===
Drug interaction information is complex and constantly evolving. This reference is for educational purposes only.
Always consult a licensed pharmacist or physician before starting, stopping, or changing any medication.
Individual patient factors (age, kidney function, liver function, other diseases) greatly affect drug interaction severity.
