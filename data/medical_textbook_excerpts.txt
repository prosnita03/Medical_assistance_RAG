=== VITAL SIGNS — NORMAL RANGES ===

Blood Pressure:
- Normal: Systolic < 120 mmHg AND Diastolic < 80 mmHg
- Elevated: Systolic 120-129 mmHg AND Diastolic < 80 mmHg
- Stage 1 Hypertension: Systolic 130-139 OR Diastolic 80-89 mmHg
- Stage 2 Hypertension: Systolic ≥ 140 OR Diastolic ≥ 90 mmHg
- Hypertensive Crisis: Systolic > 180 AND/OR Diastolic > 120 mmHg
- Hypotension: Systolic < 90 mmHg

Heart Rate:
- Normal (adults): 60-100 beats per minute (bpm)
- Bradycardia: < 60 bpm
- Tachycardia: > 100 bpm
- Athletic resting rate: May normally be 40-60 bpm

Respiratory Rate:
- Normal (adults): 12-20 breaths per minute
- Bradypnea: < 12 breaths/minute
- Tachypnea: > 20 breaths/minute

Temperature:
- Normal: 36.1-37.2°C (97-99°F) oral
- Fever: ≥ 38.0°C (100.4°F)
- Hyperthermia: > 40°C (104°F) — medical emergency
- Hypothermia: < 35°C (95°F) — medical emergency

Oxygen Saturation (SpO2):
- Normal: 95-100%
- Mild hypoxemia: 90-94%
- Moderate hypoxemia: 85-89% — supplemental oxygen required
- Severe hypoxemia: < 85% — medical emergency

BMI (Body Mass Index = weight kg / height m²):
- Underweight: < 18.5
- Normal: 18.5-24.9
- Overweight: 25.0-29.9
- Obese Class I: 30.0-34.9
- Obese Class II: 35.0-39.9
- Obese Class III (Morbid): ≥ 40.0

=== LABORATORY REFERENCE RANGES ===

Complete Blood Count (CBC):
- Hemoglobin: Male 13.5-17.5 g/dL; Female 12.0-15.5 g/dL
- Hematocrit: Male 41-53%; Female 36-46%
- White Blood Cells (WBC): 4,500-11,000 cells/μL
  - Neutrophils: 50-70% (absolute: 1,800-7,700/μL)
  - Lymphocytes: 20-40% (absolute: 1,000-4,800/μL)
  - Monocytes: 2-8%
  - Eosinophils: 1-4%
  - Basophils: 0.5-1%
- Platelets: 150,000-400,000/μL
- MCV (Mean Corpuscular Volume): 80-100 fL
- MCH: 27-33 pg
- RBC: Male 4.5-5.5 million/μL; Female 4.0-5.0 million/μL

Anemia Classification by MCV:
- Microcytic (MCV < 80): Iron deficiency anemia, thalassemia, sideroblastic anemia, anemia of chronic disease
- Normocytic (MCV 80-100): Acute blood loss, hemolytic anemia, anemia of chronic disease, aplastic anemia
- Macrocytic (MCV > 100): Vitamin B12 deficiency, folate deficiency, alcohol, liver disease, hypothyroidism, medications (methotrexate, hydroxyurea)

Basic Metabolic Panel (BMP):
- Sodium (Na): 136-145 mEq/L
- Potassium (K): 3.5-5.0 mEq/L
- Chloride (Cl): 98-107 mEq/L
- Bicarbonate (HCO3): 22-29 mEq/L
- Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN): 7-25 mg/dL
- Creatinine: Male 0.7-1.3 mg/dL; Female 0.6-1.1 mg/dL
- Glucose (fasting): 70-99 mg/dL
- Calcium: 8.5-10.5 mg/dL

Liver Function Tests (LFTs):
- ALT (Alanine aminotransferase): 7-56 U/L
- AST (Aspartate aminotransferase): 10-40 U/L
- Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP): 44-147 U/L
- Total Bilirubin: 0.2-1.2 mg/dL
- Direct (Conjugated) Bilirubin: 0.0-0.3 mg/dL
- Albumin: 3.5-5.0 g/dL
- Total Protein: 6.3-8.2 g/dL

Lipid Panel (fasting):
- Total Cholesterol: Desirable < 200 mg/dL; Borderline 200-239; High ≥ 240
- LDL Cholesterol: Optimal < 100 mg/dL; Near optimal 100-129; Borderline 130-159; High 160-189; Very high ≥ 190
- HDL Cholesterol: Low (risk) < 40 (men), < 50 (women); High (protective) ≥ 60
- Triglycerides: Normal < 150; Borderline 150-199; High 200-499; Very high ≥ 500

Thyroid Function:
- TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone): 0.4-4.0 mIU/L
- Free T4: 0.8-1.8 ng/dL
- Free T3: 2.3-4.1 pg/mL

Diabetes Markers:
- Fasting glucose: Normal 70-99 mg/dL; Prediabetes 100-125; Diabetes ≥ 126
- HbA1c: Normal < 5.7%; Prediabetes 5.7-6.4%; Diabetes ≥ 6.5%
- Target HbA1c for diabetics: Generally < 7% (ADA guidelines)
- 2-hour OGTT: Normal < 140 mg/dL; Prediabetes 140-199; Diabetes ≥ 200

Coagulation Tests:
- PT (Prothrombin Time): 11-13.5 seconds
- INR (International Normalized Ratio): Normal 0.8-1.2; Therapeutic for warfarin (DVT/PE) 2.0-3.0; Therapeutic for mechanical heart valve 2.5-3.5
- aPTT: 25-35 seconds
- Fibrinogen: 200-400 mg/dL

=== COMMON CLINICAL PRESENTATIONS ===

CHEST PAIN — Differential Diagnosis Approach:
Life-threatening causes to rule out first (RIPE mnemonic):
- Rupture (aortic dissection): Sudden severe tearing/ripping chest pain radiating to back; BP difference between arms
- Infarction (MI): Crushing/pressure, radiation to left arm/jaw, sweating, nausea; ST changes on ECG
- Pneumothorax: Sudden sharp chest pain + dyspnea; absent breath sounds; may be tension pneumothorax
- Embolism (pulmonary): Pleuritic chest pain + dyspnea + tachycardia; risk factors (immobility, surgery, DVT)

Other causes:
- Pericarditis: Sharp, positional (better leaning forward), pleuritic; friction rub on exam; diffuse ST elevation on ECG
- Esophageal spasm: Severe squeezing pain, may respond to nitroglycerin
- GERD/esophagitis: Burning, after meals, positional
- Musculoskeletal: Reproducible with palpation or movement; costochondritis
- Anxiety/panic attack: Associated with hyperventilation, palpitations, dizziness

---

SHORTNESS OF BREATH (Dyspnea) — Differential Diagnosis:
Cardiac causes:
- Acute decompensated heart failure: Orthopnea (worse lying down), PND (paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea), bilateral crackles, edema, elevated JVP
- Acute coronary syndrome: Associated chest pain, ST changes
- Cardiac tamponade: Beck's triad (hypotension, JVD, muffled heart sounds)
- Arrhythmias: Palpitations

Pulmonary causes:
- Pulmonary embolism: Sudden onset, pleuritic chest pain, risk factors for DVT
- Pneumothorax: Sudden unilateral, decreased breath sounds
- Pneumonia: Productive cough, fever, focal crackles, consolidation
- Asthma: Wheezing, reversible with bronchodilators, triggers
- COPD exacerbation: Known COPD, increased sputum, wheezing
- Pulmonary fibrosis: Chronic progressive, bibasal crackles, clubbing

Other:
- Anemia: Gradual, pallor, fatigue
- Sepsis: Fever, tachycardia, source of infection
- Metabolic acidosis: Kussmaul breathing, underlying cause
- Anxiety/panic: Hyperventilation, paresthesias, situational

---

ABDOMINAL PAIN — Location and Likely Causes:
Right Upper Quadrant (RUQ):
- Biliary colic, cholecystitis (Murphy's sign positive), cholangitis
- Hepatitis, liver abscess
- Peptic ulcer disease
- Right lower lobe pneumonia (referred pain)

Left Upper Quadrant (LUQ):
- Splenomegaly, splenic rupture
- Gastritis, peptic ulcer
- Pancreatitis (may be epigastric)
- Left lower lobe pneumonia

Epigastric:
- Peptic ulcer disease, GERD
- Pancreatitis (radiates to back)
- MI (inferior wall)
- Aortic aneurysm

Right Lower Quadrant (RLQ):
- Appendicitis (McBurney's point, Rovsing's sign)
- Ovarian cyst/torsion, ectopic pregnancy
- Inguinal hernia
- Mesenteric adenitis

Left Lower Quadrant (LLQ):
- Diverticulitis (most common in sigmoid colon)
- Ovarian cyst/torsion, ectopic pregnancy
- Inguinal hernia
- Colon pathology

Periumbilical:
- Early appendicitis (migrates to RLQ)
- Small bowel obstruction
- Mesenteric ischemia

Diffuse:
- Peritonitis (rigidity, rebound tenderness)
- IBD (Crohn's disease, ulcerative colitis)
- IBS, constipation
- Aortic aneurysm rupture

=== PREVENTIVE MEDICINE AND SCREENING GUIDELINES ===

Cancer Screening (USPSTF recommendations):
- Colorectal Cancer: Colonoscopy every 10 years starting at 45 (or earlier with risk factors); alternatives: annual FIT, stool DNA every 1-3 years, CT colonography every 5 years
- Breast Cancer: Mammography every 2 years for ages 50-74; discuss with women 40-49
- Cervical Cancer: Pap smear every 3 years ages 21-65 (or Pap + HPV co-test every 5 years ages 30-65)
- Lung Cancer: Annual low-dose CT scan for adults 50-80 years with 20 pack-year history who currently smoke or quit within past 15 years
- Prostate Cancer: Discuss PSA testing with men aged 55-69; individual decision
- Skin Cancer: Regular skin self-exams; annual dermatology visit for high-risk patients

Cardiovascular Screening:
- Blood pressure: Every 1-2 years if normal; annually if elevated
- Lipids: Every 5 years starting at 20; more frequently with risk factors
- Diabetes (HbA1c or fasting glucose): Every 3 years for adults 35-70 who are overweight/obese
- AAA screening: One-time ultrasound for male smokers/ex-smokers aged 65-75

Immunizations (Adult):
- Influenza: Annually for all adults
- Tdap/Td: Tdap once, then Td booster every 10 years
- Pneumococcal (PCV20 or PPSV23): All adults ≥ 65; younger adults with risk factors
- COVID-19: Per current CDC recommendations
- Shingles (Zoster): Shingrix (recombinant) — 2 doses for adults ≥ 50
- MMR: Two doses for non-immune adults
- Varicella: Two doses for non-immune adults
- HPV: Through age 26 (or through 45 for some individuals, shared decision-making)
- Hepatitis B: All adults up to 60; shared decision for 60+
- Hepatitis A: At-risk groups

=== COMMON MEDICAL PROCEDURES ===

ELECTROCARDIOGRAM (ECG/EKG) Interpretation — Basics:
Rate:
- Normal: 60-100 bpm
- Calculate: 300 ÷ number of large squares between R waves

Rhythm: Regular vs. irregular

P waves: Before each QRS? Normal sinus rhythm if present

Intervals:
- PR interval: Normal 120-200 ms (3-5 small squares); prolonged in heart block
- QRS duration: Normal < 120 ms (< 3 small squares); widened in bundle branch blocks
- QT interval: Normal < 440 ms (corrected for rate); prolonged in electrolyte abnormalities, medications

ST changes:
- ST elevation: Possible STEMI, pericarditis (diffuse saddle-shaped elevation), early repolarization
- ST depression: Ischemia, posterior MI, digoxin effect
- Rule: >1mm elevation in ≥2 contiguous leads

T wave changes:
- Tall peaked T waves: Hyperkalemia (tent-shaped)
- T wave inversions: Ischemia, right/left heart strain, PE

ECG Patterns:
- STEMI: ST elevation in territory (anterior = V1-V4; inferior = II, III, aVF; lateral = I, aVL, V5-V6)
- LBBB: Broad QRS with notched R in I, V5-V6; new LBBB in chest pain = treat as STEMI equivalent
- Atrial fibrillation: Irregularly irregular rhythm, no distinct P waves
- Atrial flutter: Sawtooth flutter waves at 300/min; typically 2:1 block (150 ventricular rate)
- Ventricular tachycardia: Wide complex tachycardia, regular; life-threatening

---

PAIN ASSESSMENT:
PQRST Method:
- P — Provokes/Palliates: What makes it better or worse?
- Q — Quality: Sharp, dull, aching, burning, pressure, stabbing?
- R — Radiation: Does it spread anywhere?
- S — Severity: Rate 0-10 on pain scale
- T — Time: When did it start? Constant vs. intermittent? Getting better or worse?

Pain Scales:
- Numeric Rating Scale (NRS): 0 (no pain) to 10 (worst imaginable pain)
- Visual Analog Scale (VAS): 100mm line
- Wong-Baker FACES: For children or communication-limited patients
- FLACC (Face, Legs, Activity, Cry, Consolability): For infants

=== CLINICAL PHARMACOLOGY PRINCIPLES ===

Pharmacokinetics (ADME):
Absorption:
- Bioavailability: Fraction of drug reaching systemic circulation
- Affected by: Route of administration, first-pass effect, food, pH
- IV = 100% bioavailability; oral bioavailability varies

Distribution:
- Volume of distribution (Vd): Apparent volume in which drug distributes
- Large Vd: Drug distributes to tissues (lipophilic drugs)
- Small Vd: Drug stays in bloodstream (hydrophilic drugs)
- Plasma protein binding: Bound drug is inactive; only free drug is active

Metabolism:
- Primary site: Liver (CYP450 enzymes)
- First-pass effect: Oral drugs metabolized before reaching systemic circulation
- CYP3A4: Most important; metabolizes ~50% of drugs; inhibited by grapefruit
- Prodrugs: Require metabolism to active form (e.g., codeine → morphine via CYP2D6)

Elimination:
- Half-life (t½): Time for drug concentration to decrease by 50%
- Renal clearance: Most drugs excreted by kidneys; adjust doses in renal impairment
- Steady state: Reached after 4-5 half-lives
- Creatinine clearance (CrCl): Cockcroft-Gault formula used to estimate GFR for drug dosing

Pharmacodynamics:
- Agonist: Binds receptor and activates it (full agonist vs. partial agonist)
- Antagonist: Binds receptor without activating; blocks agonist
- Competitive antagonism: Reversible; increasing agonist concentration overcomes block
- Therapeutic index (TI) = TD50/ED50; narrow TI drugs require careful monitoring

Dose-Response Relationship:
- EC50: Concentration producing 50% of maximal effect
- Loading dose: Rapidly achieves therapeutic levels = (Vd × desired concentration) / bioavailability
- Maintenance dose: Sustains steady state = (clearance × desired concentration) / bioavailability
- Therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM): Required for drugs with narrow therapeutic windows (digoxin, warfarin, lithium, aminoglycosides, vancomycin, phenytoin, cyclosporine)

=== NUTRITION AND DIETARY GUIDELINES ===

Macronutrients:
- Carbohydrates: 45-65% of total calories; complex carbs preferred; fiber 25-38g/day
- Proteins: 10-35% of total calories; 0.8g/kg body weight (minimum); increased needs in illness/pregnancy
- Fats: 20-35% of total calories; saturated fat < 10%; trans fat minimal; include omega-3 fatty acids

Micronutrients of Clinical Significance:
- Vitamin D: RDA 600-800 IU; deficiency common; needed for calcium absorption and immune function
- Calcium: 1,000-1,200 mg/day; essential for bone health
- Iron: Men 8mg/day; premenopausal women 18mg/day; deficiency = most common nutritional deficiency
- Folate: 400 mcg/day; 600 mcg during pregnancy; prevents neural tube defects
- Vitamin B12: 2.4 mcg/day; deficiency common in vegans and elderly; absorbed via intrinsic factor
- Potassium: 3,400-4,700 mg/day; important for blood pressure
- Sodium: < 2,300 mg/day; reduced intake lowers blood pressure

DASH Diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension):
- Rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean proteins, low-fat dairy
- Limits: Red meat, sweets, sugary beverages, sodium
- Shown to reduce systolic BP by 8-14 mmHg
- Also beneficial for cholesterol and diabetes management

Mediterranean Diet:
- High in: Olive oil, legumes, whole grains, fruits, vegetables, fish
- Moderate: Poultry, eggs, dairy, red wine
- Low: Red meat, processed foods
- Associated with reduced cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, cognitive decline

=== MEDICAL EMERGENCIES — QUICK REFERENCE ===

ANAPHYLAXIS:
Triggers: Medications (especially penicillin, NSAIDs, contrast), foods (nuts, shellfish, eggs), insect stings, latex
Signs: Urticaria, angioedema, bronchospasm, hypotension, nausea/vomiting
Treatment:
1. IMMEDIATE: Epinephrine 0.3-0.5mg IM (1:1000) into outer thigh (auto-injector)
2. Call emergency services
3. Lay patient flat with legs elevated (if no respiratory distress)
4. IV access, IV fluids for hypotension
5. Second epinephrine dose if needed after 5-15 minutes
6. Diphenhydramine (antihistamine), ranitidine
7. Corticosteroids (methylprednisolone) — slow onset, but prevent biphasic reaction
8. Bronchodilators for bronchospasm
9. Observe for minimum 4-6 hours (biphasic reactions can occur)

HYPOGLYCEMIA:
Definition: Blood glucose < 70 mg/dL
Symptoms: Shakiness, sweating, palpitations, anxiety (adrenergic); confusion, difficulty speaking, seizure (neuroglycopenic)
Rule of 15:
- If conscious and able to swallow: 15g fast-acting carbs (4 glucose tablets, 4oz juice, regular soda)
- Recheck glucose in 15 minutes
- Repeat if still < 70 mg/dL
- For severe/unconscious: Glucagon 1mg IM/SC, or dextrose 25g IV (50mL of D50W)

HYPERTENSIVE EMERGENCY:
Definition: Severe hypertension (>180/120) with target organ damage (encephalopathy, aortic dissection, acute MI, acute pulmonary edema, acute renal failure)
Treatment: IV antihypertensives; reduce MAP by no more than 25% in first hour, then gradual reduction over 24-48 hours
Agents: Labetalol IV, nicardipine IV, nitroprusside IV, hydralazine IV

SEIZURES:
Immediate management:
1. Protect from injury, lateral positioning
2. Do NOT restrain; do NOT put anything in mouth
3. Note time of onset
If seizure > 5 minutes (status epilepticus):
4. IV/IM benzodiazepine: Lorazepam (preferred) 4mg IV or diazepam 10mg IV
5. If refractory: Levetiracetam, valproate, phenytoin/fosphenytoin IV
6. Intubation and anesthetic coma for super-refractory status epilepticus
